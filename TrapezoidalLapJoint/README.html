Trapezoidal Lap Joint

The trapezoidal lap joint is intended to be both temporary and rigid. Constructions which use the joint can be assembled and disassembled over and over again without loosing rigidity.

I wanted to make a portable Moravian workbench but didn't want to use the tusked tennon joints that appear in most of the examples. I was concerned about the combined thickness of the wedge, the walls of its mortise, and the walls of mortise of the ultimate joint being too think.

The joint has two identical halves that each have an isosceles trapezoidal cut-out and a hole in the middle.

A wedge, cut at the same angle as the legs of the trapezoid, is glued to each cheek, just below the cut-out. When the joint is assembled, each wedge will engage with the slanted walls of the trapezoidal cut-out of the other half of the other piece.

How do we ensure that all wedges and faces meet at the same time?

Before the wedges are glued into place, the joint is screwed together with some fender washers in between the two "struts" to act as spacers. The legs are squared up to each other and the nut and bolt tightened, then the wedges are pressed and glued into place. Glue is only applied to the rectilinear faces, not the angled faces. Once the glue dries, the joint can be disassembled and the spacing washers removed. Any extra overhanging meterial can be cut from the wedges.

The spacing provided by the fender washers is important because if the joint bottoms out it can't be tightened further and will not be rigid.

When the joint is assembled and the wing nut hand tightened, it is very solid, very rigid.
